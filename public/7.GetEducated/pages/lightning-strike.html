Lightning Strike Safety Tips

What Is a Lightning Strike?

Lightning is a powerful natural electrostatic discharge that occurs during thunderstorms. A single bolt of lightning can reach temperatures of around 30,000 K (five times hotter than the surface of the sun) and carry up to one billion volts of electricity. Lightning strikes the Earth approximately 100 times per second and kills around 2,000 people worldwide each year. It is one of the most underestimated weather hazards — most lightning fatalities occur outdoors when people are caught in the open.

Warning Signs of an Imminent Lightning Strike

  • Hair standing on end: Static electricity buildup means a strike is imminent — crouch immediately.
  • Skin tingling: A tingling sensation on the skin indicates dangerous static buildup nearby.
  • Metallic taste or smell: A sharp metallic taste or the smell of ozone signals an imminent strike.
  • Dark skies and thunder: If you can hear thunder, you are already within striking distance — seek shelter immediately.
  • 30-30 rule: If the gap between lightning and thunder is less than 30 seconds, take cover. Wait 30 minutes after the last strike before going outside.

Before a Thunderstorm (Prepare)

  • Check weather forecasts before outdoor activities — postpone if thunderstorms are predicted.
  • Install surge protectors and lightning rods on your home to protect electronics and structure.
  • Trim trees near your home — falling branches during storms are a major hazard.
  • Know the nearest sturdy building or hard-topped vehicle you can take shelter in quickly.
  • Have a weather alert app or NOAA weather radio to monitor incoming storms.

During a Lightning Storm (Stay Safe)

  • Go indoors immediately — a sturdy building is the safest place.
  • Stay away from windows, doors, and porches.
  • If caught outside, crouch low on the balls of your feet — minimize contact with the ground.
  • A hard-topped vehicle with windows closed is a safe alternative to a building.
  • Never shelter under a tree — trees are the most struck objects.
  • Avoid water — lakes, rivers, pools, and even puddles conduct electricity.
  • Don't use corded phones or electrical appliances during the storm.
  • Don't lie flat on the ground — this increases your exposure to ground current.

After a Lightning Strike (First Aid)

  • Call emergency services immediately — lightning strike victims need urgent medical attention.
  • It is safe to touch a lightning strike victim — they carry no electrical charge after being struck.
  • Begin CPR immediately if the victim is unresponsive and not breathing normally.
  • Treat for shock — keep the victim warm, calm, and still until help arrives.
  • Watch for delayed symptoms — cardiac arrest, burns, neurological damage can appear hours later.

Common Lightning Myths — Busted

❌ Myth: Lightning never strikes the same place twice
✅ Fact: Lightning frequently strikes the same place multiple times — the Empire State Building is struck about 20–25 times per year.
❌ Myth: Rubber shoes protect you from lightning
✅ Fact: Rubber soles offer no meaningful protection against a billion-volt lightning bolt.
❌ Myth: You're safe under a tree
✅ Fact: Trees are the most commonly struck objects — sheltering under one is extremely dangerous.
❌ Myth: Lightning victims are electrified and dangerous to touch
✅ Fact: Lightning victims carry no electrical charge — it's completely safe to help them immediately.
